## Summary

Decouples storage from compute by offloading older log segments to a cheaper remote store (e.g. object storage) while keeping recent data on the broker's local disk.

## Motivation

Retaining data for weeks or months forces operators to over-provision broker local disk, coupling storage capacity to broker count and making scaling expensive and slow.

Most reads target recent data; historical segments sit idle on premium local disk. Moving them to cheap remote storage cuts cost and lets brokers scale on throughput, not retention.

## Proposed Changes / Design

Each log is split into a local tier (recent segments) and a remote tier. A pluggable RemoteStorageManager uploads closed segments and their indexes to the remote store; a RemoteLogMetadataManager tracks segment locations.

Fetch requests for offsets that have aged out of local storage are served transparently from the remote tier, so consumers see one continuous log regardless of where a segment physically lives.

## Trade-offs

> [!success]+ Benefits
> - Local disk sized for recent data only — dramatically lower storage cost.
> - Retention becomes near-unlimited without growing the broker fleet.
> - Faster broker recovery and reassignment: less local data to move.

> [!warning]+ Costs / Risks
> - Reads of cold data pay remote-storage latency.
> - Adds a remote store as a new dependency and failure domain.
> - Pluggable RSM/RLMM implementations must be correct and performant.

## Rejected Alternatives

**External tiering via a sidecar** — Copying segments out-of-band leaves Kafka unaware of remote data, so consumers couldn't transparently read aged-out offsets through the normal fetch path.

**Compaction-only retention** — Compaction addresses key-based dedup, not the cost of retaining a long history of all records; it doesn't solve the storage-scaling problem.
