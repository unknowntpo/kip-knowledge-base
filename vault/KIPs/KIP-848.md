## Summary

Replaces the client-driven, stop-the-world rebalance with a broker-coordinated, incremental protocol that removes global synchronization barriers.

## Motivation

The classic protocol relies on a global synchronization barrier: every member must rejoin and the whole group stalls on each membership change, causing latency spikes and cascading rebalances at scale.

Assignment logic lived in the clients, so fixing bugs or changing strategy meant upgrading every consumer — painful across polyglot fleets.

## Proposed Changes / Design

The group coordinator on the broker computes assignments and drives reconciliation. Members send their current state via a heartbeat and receive incremental target assignments.

Reconciliation is incremental: only the partitions that actually move are revoked and reassigned, and members that are unaffected keep processing — no group-wide stop-the-world.

## Trade-offs

> [!success]+ Benefits
> - No stop-the-world; unaffected members keep consuming during a rebalance.
> - Assignment logic on the broker — upgrade once, not every client.
> - Scales to much larger consumer groups with less disruption.

> [!warning]+ Costs / Risks
> - New protocol coexisting with the old one during a long migration window.
> - More coordinator-side CPU and state.
> - Client and tooling ecosystem must add support incrementally.

## Rejected Alternatives

**Incrementally patch the old protocol** — Cooperative rebalancing (KIP-429) already softened revocation, but the global JoinGroup barrier is structural and can't be removed without a new protocol.

**Fully client-side redesign** — Keeping assignment in clients perpetuates the upgrade-everyone problem and can't leverage the coordinator's global view.
